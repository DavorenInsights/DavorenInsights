Not Every Electric Tractor Should Earn Carbon Credits

That is not anti-climate. It is pro-integrity.

Carbon Markets Climate Finance Additionality

Take a farm as a simple example. A diesel tractor is replaced by an electric tractor. Now there is a serious case to assess: What is the baseline? What is being displaced? Was there a real emissions reduction? Would this shift have happened anyway, or did carbon finance help make it viable?

Now take a different case. A new farm starts with an electric tractor from day one. That may still be the right climate decision. But it does not automatically mean the project should earn carbon credits. Why? Because low carbon is not the same as creditable.

Where additionality matters

A carbon credit should represent a reduction that is real, measurable, and linked to a credible baseline — not simply a good-looking technology choice.

The real question is not: "Is this electric?" It is: what exactly is being credited, against what baseline, and under what justification? That is the line between real climate finance and weak climate claims.

The integrity problem

Used badly, carbon credits become a loose label attached to almost anything with a green story. Used well, they can help finance difficult transitions that are not yet commercially easy.

The real discussion is not whether carbon credits are good or bad. It is whether they are being used in a way that genuinely accelerates transition without weakening integrity.

Not every electric tractor should earn credits. But in the right case, a high-integrity credit can help move capital from the old system to a more climate-focused one.
