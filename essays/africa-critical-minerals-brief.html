Endowment Is Not Strategy

Africa holds 30% of global transition minerals and captures a fraction of the value. The constraint is structural, compounding — and the window is closing faster than any continental strategy document admits.

Critical Minerals Africa Energy Transition Development Finance

Core argument

Africa's binding constraint is not geological. It is the persistent failure to assemble energy, logistics, capital, technology access, and feedstock sovereignty simultaneously.

The deadline

Battery and copper demand peaks around 2035, then moderates as recycling scales. Projects not in advanced development by 2026–27 will not reach full production before peak primary demand.

What breaks the thesis

China controls 72%+ of DRC cobalt and copper mines, and is now restricting export of the processing technologies Africa needs to build alternatives. Processing geography is diverging from control of value.

Decision implication

Strategies calibrated to 2030 implementation timelines are already marginal. Corridor-scale financing, not project-level DFI approval, is the instrument that could still make the difference.

The endowment–capture gap

Africa accounts for over 10% of global production in fourteen green transition minerals — cobalt (73%), manganese (65%), PGMs (57%), chromium (56%), copper (16%). The IEA values Africa's mining output at approximately USD 50 billion and refining at USD 16 billion. That ratio is the structural problem in a single number.

The production-to-refining gap is consistent across every mineral. Africa produces 72% of global cobalt but refines 8%. It produces 65% of manganese but refines 0% to battery grade. 22% of graphite — effectively 0% refined. This is not a consequence of insufficient endowment. It is a consequence of insufficient energy, logistics, technology access, cost of capital, and market structure — consistently, everywhere, across all minerals simultaneously.

Four cascades decision-makers miss

The endowment gap is widely documented. What continental strategy documents largely miss is the chain of compounding effects when first-order conditions shift.

A Geography of production vs. ownership of value
1st order

Indonesia diversified nickel refining geographically — nearly 45% of global refined nickel by location in 2024.

2nd order

Chinese companies own ~65% of Indonesian nickel refining. Indonesian companies own 10%. Geography diverged completely from control of value.

3rd order

This is the Indonesia lesson African strategy documents cite as a model but do not absorb. China controls 72%+ of cobalt and copper mines in DRC. African processing built on Chinese investment reproduces the pattern — Africa gains production statistics, China retains the margin.

B Chemistry shift meets technology lock
1st order

LFP captured nearly half the global EV battery market by 2024. Projected cobalt 2040 demand is already being revised down. Manganese-rich chemistries are the next growth direction.

2nd order

African countries rationally reorient toward LFP-aligned minerals — manganese, phosphate, lithium. DRC cobalt strategies face a shrinking addressable market.

3rd order

In January 2025, China proposed export licence restrictions on LFP cathode production technology, REE separation technology, and lithium refining technology. At the precise moment chemistry change creates an African entry opportunity, China is closing the technology transfer channel. No continental strategy document has addressed this constraint.

C Price decline as structural sorting mechanism
1st order

Battery metal prices fell sharply in 2023–24. Cobalt fell 60%+ from its 2022 peak. Lithium collapsed. Graphite and nickel declined significantly.

2nd order

Low prices disproportionately damage prospective projects outside main incumbent producers. African projects carry higher WACC, less integrated supply chains, and no state subsidy at Chinese scale.

3rd order

Each price cycle in which African entry projects fail to survive makes the next entry attempt structurally harder. The 2023–24 environment was a selection event that reinforced concentration. When prices recover, the competitive gap has widened.

D Exploration underinvestment and the 2035 window
1st order

African mineral exploration budgets have declined since 2011 and remain below Latin American levels. Recorded reserves likely underestimate actual endowment.

2nd order

With a 16–29 year average development lag, the decision to explore now determines whether Africa can supply the 2040–2050 market.

3rd order

Countries exceeding the investment attractiveness threshold — Botswana, Morocco, South Africa, Ghana, Namibia — are capturing disproportionate exploration capital. Australia's WA Exploration Incentive Scheme returns A$23.70 per A$1 invested. Africa has no continental equivalent at operating scale.

Core decision trade-offs

Every viable strategy path involves explicit trade-offs. The table below names what breaks when each is pursued in isolation.

Speed to processing
What breaks

Only capital available at speed and scale is Chinese. Accepting it on Chinese terms reproduces the Indonesia pattern: geographic production statistics, Chinese ownership of value.

Second-order risk

A decade of infrastructure locked into Chinese offtake before Western diversification capital arrives — if it arrives. The 2035 peak passes during the lock-in period.

National processing sovereignty
What breaks

Most countries lack feedstock breadth, energy platform, or market proximity for standalone viability. The McKinsey minimum scale — 10–15 kt/year at USD 0.5–1.5bn — is beyond most single-country pipelines.

Second-order risk

Capital consumed in national studies and MOUs while the regional platform that could achieve scale goes unfunded. Policy effort is high; industrial output is zero.

Export restrictions as leverage
What breaks

Works only where market share is sufficient that buyers cannot substitute. DRC cobalt (73% share) and SA/Gabon manganese jointly (60%+) are the rare cases. Indonesia's bauxite ban failed; its nickel ban succeeded.

Second-order risk

Restrictions without processing infrastructure ready to absorb feedstock shift trade flows, damage buyer relations, and reduce fiscal revenues without industrial benefit.

NMC / cobalt-centric strategy
What breaks

LFP holds nearly half the EV battery market. Projected cobalt 2040 demand is already being revised downward. Processing infrastructure commissioned in 2025 enters a structurally shifting market.

Second-order risk

Stranded processing assets. Investment in cobalt hydroxide or NMC precursor capacity as a primary strategy locks in exposure to a declining market segment.

Three forward signals

Signal 01 2035 is the deadline, not 2050

The World Bank Mineral Resources of Africa study finds that battery and copper demand peaks around 2035, then moderates as recycling ramps up. This is absent from every continental strategy document reviewed. Processing projects not in advanced development by 2026–27 will not reach full production before peak primary demand. Strategies calibrated to 2035 implementation are too late for primary supply.

Signal 02 China is locking technology as chemistry opens mineral opportunity

China's January 2025 export restrictions on LFP cathode production technology, REE separation technology, and lithium refining technology cover patent rights, licensing, and technical services — closing virtually all transfer pathways simultaneously. The strategic response requires identifying alternative technology pathways — Korean manufacturers with independently developed LFP, Hyundai/Kia and Nano One precursor-free processes, non-acid graphite purification — before restrictions become permanent barriers. No African government or DFI has yet named this as a priority.

Signal 03 Geological survey infrastructure is the binding upstream constraint that no strategy document prioritises

AI-assisted mineral targeting — demonstrated at scale by KoBold Metals' Mingomba discovery — is commercially viable now. The binding constraint is data quality and skills, not the technology. African geological surveys with open, digitised, GIS-based databases translate directly into exploration investment at measured returns. This is the least glamorous and most underfunded strategic lever in the ecosystem.

Strategic implication

Africa's critical minerals challenge is not a shortage of endowment, strategy documents, or political ambition. It is the persistent failure to assemble the enabling conditions for processing viability — affordable energy, functional logistics, sovereign feedstock control, competitive cost of capital, technology access, and market proximity — simultaneously, at the scale required, within the available time window.

The corridor and platform model — of which Lobito is the most advanced real example — is the only architecture currently visible in the evidence that assembles enough enabling conditions simultaneously to approach viability at scale. It requires financing at corridor scale rather than project scale, DFI mandate expansion rather than project approval, and technology partnership negotiation rather than passive reliance on transfer.

Everything else risks being well-designed strategy for a window that has already begun to close.
